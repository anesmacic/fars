Fatality Analysis Reporting System (FARS-2018)¶

of the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration of the U.S. Department of Transportation¶

TASK¶

The CEO of GCRoc would like to try to improve their pricing and product offerings for automobile insurance. They have traditionally only operated in the North East but would like to go nationwide. She gives you data on fatal automobile accidents from 2011 and asks you to “see what the data tell you.” GCRoc is still seeking approval for their expansion from the regulators and may only be allowed to add one additional Census region at a time.

Formulating a question in efforts to gear EDA¶

About the dataset¶

The FARS (2015-2018) dataset contains data of all motor vehicle accidents (MVAs) that resulted in at least 1 fatality. For every fatality, data about the persons, vehicles and other relevant factors were included. The state of the data is not optimal as there are many missing fields. As insurance revenue is generated primarily from insuring drivers and not paying out claims, it is important to note that this dataset does not include MVAs that did not result in a fatality and therefor provides no ratio of how many total accidents each region/state has per driver.

Question¶

The main goal of this analysis is to identify geographic patterns of risk factors/identifiers of fatalities. As no information can be inferred about a possible insurance payout per driver, the analysis must be geared towards identifying factors relevant to death/fatalities for which a higher possible insurance payout per driver is possible. In other words, as there is no way of telling how many claims a region might have, this analysis attempts to identify possible fatality claims (claims that result in a higher possible insurance payout due to death) and to identify any geographical patterns in their identifiers (such as medical response time, alcohol levels, urban-rural ratio of fatal MVAs, speed limits, age, person type, air bag (mis)use etc.).

This analysis therefor may only pertain to evaluating how costly a fatality is for the insurance company (more precisely: does it vary regionally?) and all recommendations included here should be taken into consideration with the total number of MVAs to determine what region is the most profitable to expand into, should that data become available.

def MapKeys2States2Regiona(state,stateDictionary,regionDictionary,regionIndex):
    state = stateDictionary[state]
    for _,region in regionDictionary.items():
        if (state in region):
            for key,values in regionDictionary.items():
                if region==values:
                    return regionIndex[key]

def populate_response_time(data):
    response_time = data.apply(lambda x: 
                                        calculate_response_time(
                                            x
                                        ) 
     
                               if time_valid(x) else time_valid_sub(x)
                                        ,
                                        axis=1)
    data['RESPONSE_TIME'] = response_time
    
def calculate_response_time(row):
    HOSP_HR, NOT_HOUR, HOSP_MIN, NOT_MIN = row[['HOSP_HR','NOT_HOUR','HOSP_MN','NOT_MIN']]
    
    if HOSP_HR < NOT_HOUR:
        hour_diff = HOSP_HR+23-NOT_HOUR # interval crossing midnight

    elif HOSP_HR == NOT_HOUR:
        hour_diff = 0
    else:
        hour_diff = HOSP_HR - NOT_HOUR -1
        
    if hour_diff >= 0:
        min_diff = HOSP_MIN + (60-NOT_MIN)
    else:
        min_diff = HOSP_MIN - NOT_MIN

    result = hour_diff*60+min_diff 
    
    return result if result < 180 else 180


def time_valid(row):
    
    if row['HOSP_HR'] > 23 or row['NOT_HOUR'] > 23 or row['HOSP_MN'] > 59 or row['NOT_MIN'] > 59:
        return False
    return True

def time_valid_sub(row):
    if row['HOSP_HR'] < 23 or row['NOT_HOUR'] < 23:
        if row['HOSP_MN'] > 59 or row['NOT_MIN'] > 59:
            return -1  # instead of dropping these let's fill them in as -1 instead of NaN
        else:
            return (row['HOSP_HR'] - row['NOT_HOUR'])*60 
        # when the minutes are missing we'll assume the full length of an hour, but will check 
        # how this affects the distribution
    else:
        return -1 # instead of dropping these let's fill them in as -1 instead of NaN

If we sort our association rules by lift (or even by leverage and conviction) we see a byproduct of something we have seen earlier - class imbalance. Our dataset is heavily slanted with MVAs from the South region and consequently our association rules. If we drop out Region_3 from our association rules, nothing remains. We set our support confidence to be at least 20% to get around the class imbalance. The same could be obtained if we sample for balance. Let's stop for a minute here. Sometimes putting everything down the drain and seeing what happens is a valid approach but right now we should stop and ask ourseleves how exactly are we going about this?

Why are we look at the centroids? What exactly are we looking for? We are indeed looking to cluster our data well, but the only meaningful result we can obtain is if we cluster them with boundaries that also cleave them regionally. We still care about the boundaries (we want them to be separable otherwise how can we say they are two separate clusters) but we care more that they are regionally homogeneous so that will be our number 1 metric -> measuring regional homogeneity.

Let's look at the mean sum of squares of our samples and see if there's a cluster range for which the elbow metric deems good.

So for every number of clusters we will do the following:

  1. perform K-means
  2. (optional) produce a silhouette chart to see how separable the clusters are 
         this might take a while as our dataset is relatively large for an algorithm like this
  3. produce a homogeneity matrix: 
         H(n_cluster) = n_cluster x 4-regions
     --> for every cluster measure the contribution of each region 
         --> good regional homogeneity is 1 0 0 0 (one major region in a cluster)
         --> bad regional homogeneity is 0.25 0.25 0.25 0.25 (all regions equally present -> no conclusion)

Now let's turn to supervised learning. Sometimes it is hard to reformulate our question that essentially is a question for unsupervised learning into something that a supervised learning algorithm can answer.

In this case we can choose a method that provides insight into how it's making its decision - decision trees (or random forests or any ensemble version). We can provide our target variable to be the region we are looking for and see whether main features that decrease the entropy (or any other split criterion) differ among the regions. We also need to look at how good our model is so that we can judge whether the comparison is valid and to do that we should penalize for both false positives as well as false negatives. Since our dataset will naturally be class imbalanced (as we're only looking at one region at a time) some kind of a trade-off between metrics is needed and what's better than looking at our F-score?

CONCLUSION for the insurance company¶

As our goal was to find an indicator (or a set of indicators) that resulted in worse or larger number of fatalities per million drivers in a specific region, we used different algorithms to find groups of indicators that frequently occur together in our data and then filter them out into those that contain or indicate a region. Once we filtered them out, no frequent patterns were observed. In other words, this method told us that there are frequent patterns in our data (such as restraint misuse and speeding that come together with alcohol use) but those patterns happen in all regions fairly equally. Next, we took a look at how much each fatality resembles another and try to cluster similar fatalities and see if the regional cleavage will follow that clustering. This data did not show clustering of similar indicators of fatal MVAs that aligned with regional clustering. Next we turned to our models that are predictive and learn on data in efforts to see whether what they learnt on each region differs and our results show that it doesn't. In other words, it is hard to say what region a fatal MVA belongs to.

We then proceeded to extract and infer data based on attributes given in the raw dataset. We were able to extract the medical response time [MRT] (the time needed to arrive at the hospital after the crash) and to see if that differed regionally and if so, is there more data to explain the difference and/or to account for possible increased risk of fatality? We assumed this would be useful information to you as you could estimate the financial risk of having regionally poorer transportation that could result in regionally higher death claims.

What we noticed on the FARS dataset is that the distribution of the MRT has two distinct peaks whose ratios correspond to the ratios of urban versus rural fatal MVAs. In other words, the regions that see more urban fatal MVAs, associate smaller MRTs (the first peak) as the hospitals are closer to the place of accident. For the Western region this wasn’t the case as the peaks behaved paradoxically i.e. even though there was significantly more urban fatal MVAs, the second peak (the one that shows cases that took much longer time) was significantly more emphasized. To rule out the possibility of statistical insignificance, we took a look at the datasets for the previous 4 years and found the same behavior. No other indicators were noticeably different between Western and other regions. In an attempt to explain this behavior, we tried to visualize whether the cases that correspond to the second peak were in fact urban and not rural. Indeed, we found out that for urban fatal MVAs in the Western region the average medical response time was higher than for other regions. However the meaning of this is not clear as it can mean that the emergency transport services (ETS) are slower and thus result in more urban fatalities but it can also mean that the road infrastructure is different in the West and that the hospitals are simply a bit more further out of the urban areas than in other regions.

Why this information is not actionable?¶

Once you normalize the number of fatal MVAs with the number of drivers for all states, the distribution is uniform across all states. If it is indeed the case that for the Western region, the ETS are slower and thus a risk indicator, then there is something that prevents fatalities in the same quantity as that increases as it regionally levels out to national fatality rate. Since we have no data on prevented or nonfatal MVAs we cannot know whether that is profitable to your case. Therefor this anomaly is not actionable as more research must be put towards understanding it.

Main takeaways and conclusions¶

This data demonstrates no regional cleavage in risk indicators of fatal motor vehicle accidents. An anomaly was observed in the relationship between Medical Response Time and urban/rural fatal MVA ratio in the Northern region. This however could but does not necessarily indicate a potential risk indicator for the fatality of MVAs. We recommend that in making your decision, the dataset that contains all MVAs be analyzed so that a better conclusion may be reached as this dataset may only explain how ‘costly’ a fatality may be. Our results show that this cost does not vary regionally.
